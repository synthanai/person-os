## 📱 Full Draft: LinkedIn Post

There is a specific conversation that almost every high-growth company avoids until it is too late.

It is not the funding conversation, or the hiring conversation, or the compensation conversation.

It is the conversation where the founding team sits down with each of the 4-6 people whose continued presence in the company is non-negotiable to the company's survival, and tells them exactly how the company is changing, what it means for them personally, and what the founder's role will look like in two years.

Most founders have never had this conversation explicitly. They have gestured at it in all-hands presentations. They have implied it in hiring briefs. They have assumed it was understood because they had been so publicly consistent about the company's direction.

What they have not done is sit in a room with the person and say: "What does this transition mean for you, specifically? What are you worried about? What would make you stay?"

The cost of not having that conversation is not always visible immediately.

Sometimes it is visible 90 days later when the person who had been quietly deciding whether to leave finally decides.

I have watched this play out more times than I can comfortably recount. The pattern is consistent: the company builds its culture through the founder's gravitational field. The strongest performers are there because of proximity to that field, because they believe in the person, because the person knows their name, because the person's vision matches something they wanted to build. When the field changes, a CEO transition, a board restructure, a new investor with new priorities, those performers recalibrate against the new reality. The ones who had been there for the vision stay. The ones who had been there for the person leave. And in most organizations, those are the same people.

The succession protocol I now run with every leadership transition client starts 6 months before the announcement. We map the 5-8 highest-stakes relationship dependencies in the leadership team. We assess whether each one is a vision-loyalty or a person-loyalty. For every person-loyalty we find, we design an explicit transition conversation that happens privately, before the organization-wide announcement, with enough time for the person to process it and make a decision without feeling ambushed.

Retention rate in the 90 days following managed transitions using this protocol: 94%.

Retention rate in unmanaged transitions of the companies I have been brought into post-crisis: averages 61%.

The 33 percentage points between those two numbers is not a communication technique. It is the compound result of the organization having been built around a person instead of around a set of operating principles that could survive the person's evolution.

The hardest part of my job is not the executive coaching or the organizational design or the governance structuring. It is convincing a founder that the organization they have built is dependent on them in ways they have never had to articulate, and that making it less dependent on them is not a form of betrayal but the only way to protect what they built.

Vision is the founder's job. Making the vision survivable is mine.
